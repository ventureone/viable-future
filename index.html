The Viable Future

preserving the space of possible futures

A system can become more efficient, more coordinated, more predictable — and less able to change. What appears as strength can be a contraction. This game is about that contraction.

What you are looking at

A graph of possible futures. The dark canvas is the void — everywhere the graph is not. Deep green nodes are interior: futures buffered from the void by layers of other futures. Amber nodes are boundary: they face the void directly. The colored gradient is the depth of your viability.

Your task is not to grow the graph or to win. It is to preserve the interior — the space of futures that remain reopenable under perturbation. This is tracked as H(t), the sum of every node's depth. Watch the two charts. When H(t) falls, you have fewer genuinely different futures available, even if the structure looks intact.

Your four choices

WeaveBridge two distant regions. Thickens the interior through connection. If the region you bridge is already cohesive enough, growth may happen on its own at the frontier. Growth is earned, not chosen.
FearHarden the most exposed region. Freezes boundary nodes and shields them from erosion. Safer in the short term. But frozen nodes cannot participate in future weaving — protection always narrows.
CompressionPrune the weakest nodes. Feels clean, intelligent, clarifying. This is the action that feels best. It is also the fastest way to thin the future.
RegretReopen something recently closed. Thaws frozen nodes, or recovers losses before they sediment permanently. A repair channel, not a punishment.

The background process

While you choose, sedimentation runs quietly. Unused paths grow brittle. Unvisited nodes lose activity and eventually freeze. Ghost losses beyond the recovery window become permanent. You cannot turn this off. It is the cost of existing in irreversible time.

The ending

The game does not end in a crash. It ends when H(t) reaches zero — when every surviving node is boundary, when no depth remains. The graph may still exist. It may still look ordered. But nothing can change.

Choose your mode of action
You stand before a graph of possible futures. The deep green center is the interior — futures still buffered from the void. The amber edge is boundary, where the graph meets what is not. Choose how you want to relate to this.

The structure you just watched collapse is not imaginary. It describes what happens to organizations that streamline for efficiency, to people who narrow their lives in the name of stability, to systems that optimize their way into brittleness.

Sedimentation death does not look like failure. It looks like orderly continuation — until the capacity to become otherwise is gone, and by then it is too late to notice.

The question your system keeps asking is not what are you achieving, but what futures are you still able to move into. Those are different questions. Most systems only measure the first.
